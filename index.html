PhD Stuff (at ISU): Aug 2017 - current

  • Thesis Research: I am pursuing my PhD under Prof Jin Tian. My research revolves around learning Bayesian Networks from data. My thesis would be (as per current plan) to learn m-graph [Ref: K Mohan 2013] from missing data: i.e., given a data containing missing values, devise techniques to learn Bayesian Networks where variables causing missingness are also present.
    Currently however I am trying to tackle a bit simpler problem, with no missing data, wherein I am working with different search spaces to learn Bayesian Network more efficiently. Specifically I am exploring if search in partition space [Ref: J. Kuipers and G. Moffa], could give better/faster results as compared to ordered space [Ref: Mare and D Koller 2005; Muro 2015]. Specifically, I am trying to adapt code present here, by mauro-idsia.
  • Research Assistant work: Working with Dr Adisak Sukul on NSF funded project. Project involves: automated collection and coding of online campaign advertising. It has four major steps:
    1. Collecting YouTube ads from different US states with different age and sex profiles (currently on this step).
    2. Classifying the political and nonpolitical ads.
    3. Applying NLP and computer vision to extract several details such as content and category (attack vs promotion vs contrast) [Ref: Lei Qi.].
    4. Analyzing the data to estimate the amount of spending, to clarify the user profiling, and so on.

This was an amazing book. I always wondered, why rereading a paper many times in one seating didn't give me the kind of insights which I get when I read it after some time (days/weeks). As per book, during NREM sleep, day to day memories from hippocampus, are transferred to neocortex (permanent storage). This process is quite active and I believe some form of indexing ect also happens. Thus next time when we read the same thing we are making new connections which is often not possible, no matter how hard we try in the first go. Cracking this code completely would be awesome.
